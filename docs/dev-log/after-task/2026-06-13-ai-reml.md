## Implementation

`fit_ai_reml` is a Newton-type REML optimizer. Per iteration it (1) solves the
sparse Henderson MME, (2) reads the variance-component **score** from the BLUP
solution and the Takahashi selected inverse — the score matches the validated
EM fixed point: `s_a = -½σa⁻²(q·σa − tr(A⁻¹C^uu) − u'A⁻¹u)`,
`s_e = -½σe⁻²(σe(n−p−q+tr(A⁻¹C^uu)/σa) − e'e)`; (3) builds the
**average-information matrix** `AIij = ½ wi' P wj` from the working variates
`w_a = Zû/σa`, `w_e = ê/σe` via two MME re-solves that reuse the same Cholesky
factor; (4) takes an AI/Newton step `θ += AI⁻¹ s` with step-halving to keep the
variances positive (and a small ridge if AI is near-singular). REML-only,
2-component, Gaussian.

## Why this works here when DRM.jl's AI-REML did not

The forensic investigation (`wf_81a0948a`; DRM commits `a5bcb3f`, `f2ee141`;
DRM after-task `2026-06-12-observed-info-newton-reml.md`) found DRM **built then
reverted** AI-REML: the Gilmour–Thompson–Cullis average-information identity
holds only *in expectation* over a draw with covariance `H⁻¹`, but DRM's
**augmented-state Laplace** model has a shrunk inner mode whose covariance is
~5× too small, so its realized AI metric was undersized (measured AI 10.0 vs
observed Hessian 22.2 vs expected-info 51.8) and Newton diverged. They renamed
`_glsp_aireml_*` → `_glsp_reml_*` "for honesty" and shipped observed-information
Newton instead.

The HSquared animal model is the **exact Gaussian LMM**, where the AI matrix uses
the data directly and is a valid metric. Confirmed with DRM's own diagnostic: the
AI matrix matches the observed information at the optimum (**ratio 0.988 / 0.986**
on a 250-animal sim), not undersized. **Lesson for later phases:** if HSquared
reaches non-Gaussian / GLLVM-style families (Phase 6), AI-REML will break the same
way and observed-information Newton (DRM's solution) is the reuse path.

## Fastest-CPU-REML — first benchmark (directive task #5)

Simulated animal model, one record each, warmed JIT:

| N | AI-REML | EM-REML | NelderMead |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 250 | 10 iters, 51 ms | 169 iters, 354 ms | **30 ms** |
| 1000 | 7 iters, **386 ms** | 195 iters, 11.4 s | 645 ms |

Honest reading: **AI-REML wins at scale, NelderMead wins on small problems** —
AI-REML's per-iteration overhead (a selected inversion + working-variate solves)
only pays off once NelderMead's iteration count grows. EM-REML is correct but
far slower (linear convergence). This is a first data point (Gaussian, 2
components, CPU); a fuller size/family sweep + GPU are the remaining directive
work.
